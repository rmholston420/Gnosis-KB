# Impermanence and Change

*Anicca* — impermanence — is the first of the three marks of existence in Theravāda teaching. Nothing that arises through causes and conditions can persist unchanged. This is not a pessimistic observation; it is a precise description of how phenomena work.

## The Practical Instruction

When we resist impermanence, we suffer. When we hold an experience — pleasant or painful — as if it were permanent, we set up a contraction in the mind: grasping if it is pleasant, aversion if it is painful. The practice is to meet each arising with open awareness and let it move through without the story of permanence.

> *Whatever is impermanent is suffering. Whatever is impermanent and suffering is not mine, not I, not my self.*
> — SN 22.59, Anattalakkhaṇa Sutta

## Impermanence in the Vajrayāna Context

In the Tibetan tradition, impermanence contemplation (*mi rtag pa*) is the first of the Four Thoughts that Turn the Mind. It is not a preliminary to be passed through quickly but a view to be stabilised deeply enough that it informs every moment of practice.

The recognition that this moment, this body, this opportunity to practise will not last is what transforms the teaching from philosophy into urgency.

## Working with Impermanence in Daily Life

1. **Notice transitions.** Each moment you move from one activity to another is a small death of the previous state. Acknowledge it.
2. **Use the breath.** Each exhale is a letting go. Each inhale is a new arising. The breath is the most accessible teacher of impermanence.
3. **Contemplate at end of day.** Before sleep: *what has changed today that I assumed would stay the same?*
