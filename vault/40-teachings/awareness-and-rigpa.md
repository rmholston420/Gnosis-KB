# Awareness and Rigpa

*Rigpa* (Tibetan: རིག་པ) is the Dzogchen term for the recognition of the nature of mind — non-dual, luminous, open awareness that is the ground of all experience. It is distinguished from ordinary awareness (*sem*) in that it is not a *state* produced by meditation but the recognition of what is always already the case.

## The Distinction: Rigpa vs. Sem

| | Sem (ordinary mind) | Rigpa (awareness) |
|---|---|---|
| Nature | Dualistic, concept-bound | Non-dual, self-knowing |
| Arises | Through causes and conditions | Primordially present |
| Obscured by | Grasping, aversion, ignorance | Temporarily, as clouds obscure sky |
| Practice | Purification and development | Recognition and stabilisation |

## The Introduction to Rigpa

In the Dzogchen tradition, the teacher introduces the student directly to rigpa — not through gradual steps but through *pointing-out instructions* (*ngo sprod*). The classic teaching is the threefold sky: the outer sky, the inner sky of awareness, and the secret sky of rigpa are recognised as not-two.

## Stabilising the Recognition

Recognition (*ngo shes pa*) is only the beginning. The practice is to return to that recognition, again and again, through:
- **Trekchö** — cutting through: resting in the naturalness of rigpa without modification
- **Tögal** — direct crossing: working with the luminous display of awareness

## The Relevance of This to a Knowledge System

A personal knowledge base like Gnosis mirrors the structure of mind: notes arise, link, cluster, and dissolve. The watcher of the system — the one who reads and connects — is the practitioner. The practice of noting thought in meditation and the practice of capturing thought in a Zettelkasten are structurally similar: both slow the movement of mind enough to see what is actually arising.
