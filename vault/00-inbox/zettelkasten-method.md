# Zettelkasten Method

The Zettelkasten (German: *slip box*) is a note-taking method developed by sociologist Niklas Luhmann, who used it to produce an extraordinary output of 70 books and 400 scholarly articles. The method treats knowledge as a network of atomic notes, not a hierarchy of folders.

## Core Principles

1. **Atomicity** — Each note contains exactly one idea. If you find yourself writing "and also…" the note should be split.
2. **Connectivity** — Every note links to at least one other note. A note with no links is not in the system; it's just a document.
3. **Own words** — Never copy-paste. Re-state ideas in your own language; this forces comprehension.
4. **Unique IDs** — Notes are identified by timestamp or sequence number, not titles, so titles can be changed without breaking links.

## Three Types of Notes

- **Fleeting notes** — Quick captures; processed within 1–2 days or discarded
- **Literature notes** — What you take from a source, in your own words
- **Permanent notes** — Atomic concept notes written for your future self

## Why It Works

Traditional note-taking produces silos. The Zettelkasten produces a *thought network*. When you link a new note to existing ones, you are forced to ask: *how does this idea relate to what I already know?* That question is where learning happens.
