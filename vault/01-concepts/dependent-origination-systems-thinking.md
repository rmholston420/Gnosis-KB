# Dependent Origination and Systems Thinking

*Pratītyasamutpāda* — dependent origination — is the Buddha's central ontological teaching: all phenomena arise in dependence upon conditions; nothing exists independently.

Systems science arrives at a structurally identical conclusion through a different path: complex systems are defined by their *relations*, not their elements. Remove the web of relations and you do not have the system's parts — you have nothing that the system *was*.

## The Core Parallel

| Buddhist Framework | Systems Science Analog |
|---|---|
| Dependent origination | Relational ontology |
| No inherent existence (*niḥsvabhāva*) | No isolated components |
| The Twelve Nidānas (causal chain) | Feedback loops |
| *Indra's Net* metaphor | Network topology |
| Middle Way (neither existence nor non-existence) | Emergence (neither reducible nor mystical) |

## Key Insight

Both frameworks resist the twin errors of *essentialism* (things have fixed inner natures) and *nihilism* (things don't really exist at all). The middle path runs through *process* and *relation*.

This means causal analysis must always be circular — every cause is itself an effect of prior conditions. Linear causality is an abstraction valid locally but not globally.

## Open Questions
- Does the Buddhist analysis of causality (4 conditions: *hetu*, *pratyaya*, *ālambanapratyaya*, *samanantarapratyaya*) map onto Aristotle's four causes in a systems-theoretic way?
- Can formal causal modeling (e.g., Judea Pearl's *do*-calculus) illuminate the Twelve Nidānas?
