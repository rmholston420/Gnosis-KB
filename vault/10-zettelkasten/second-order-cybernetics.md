# Second-Order Cybernetics

First-order cybernetics (Wiener, Shannon) studied **the control and communication of systems**. Second-order cybernetics, developed primarily by Heinz von Foerster in the late 1960s and 1970s, turns the lens around: it is the **cybernetics of cybernetics** — the study of observing systems, not just observed systems.

The key shift: **the observer is included in the domain of observation**. You cannot describe a system from a god's-eye view outside it; every description is made by an observer who is also a system, also embedded in an environment.

## Core Concepts

### Eigenvalues of Experience
Von Foerster proposed that stable perceptions — what we call "objects" — are **eigenbehaviours** of the sensorimotor system. Reality, in this view, is not discovered but computed by the nervous system's recursive interactions with its environment. This resonates with Francisco Varela's concept of enaction.

### The Principle of Undifferentiated Encoding
Nerve cells cannot encode the nature of a stimulus — only its intensity. The nervous system is **operationally closed**: signals within it are undifferentiated with respect to their external cause. What we see, hear, and touch is our construction, not a transparent window onto the world.

### Trivial vs. Non-Trivial Machines
- **Trivial machine**: same input always produces same output (deterministic, predictable)
- **Non-trivial machine**: output depends on internal state, which changes with each operation (history-dependent, unpredictable)

Living systems — and [[zettelkasten-method|Zettelkasten slip-boxes]] — are non-trivial machines.

## Ethics and Epistemology

Von Foerster's ethical imperative: *"Act always so as to increase the number of choices."* This follows from the epistemology: if reality is constructed, then **we are responsible for the realities we construct**.
