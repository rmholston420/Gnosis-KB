# Epistemology and Justified Belief

Epistemology is the branch of philosophy concerned with the nature, sources, and limits of knowledge. The classical definition of knowledge — **justified true belief** (JTB) — was dominant from Plato's Meno until Edmund Gettier's 1963 paper showed it was insufficient.

## The JTB Account

S knows that P if and only if:
1. P is true
2. S believes that P
3. S is justified in believing that P

## The Gettier Problem

Gettier constructed cases where all three conditions are met but we would not say the subject *knows*. Example: you glance at a stopped clock that happens to show the correct time. You have a true, justified belief about the time — but you don't know it.

This launched a massive research program to find a fourth condition or to replace JTB entirely. Candidates include:
- **Reliabilism** (Goldman): belief is knowledge if formed by a reliable cognitive process
- **Virtue epistemology**: knowledge requires the exercise of intellectual virtues
- **Contextualism**: what counts as knowledge depends on conversational context

## Relevance to Knowledge Management

The JTB debate has practical consequences for [[zettelkasten-method|personal knowledge systems]]. When we write a permanent note, we are implicitly claiming something like knowledge. Reliabilism suggests we should attend to the **sources** of our beliefs — peer-reviewed, replicable research vs. blog posts vs. hearsay — and flag epistemic confidence explicitly.

## Buddhist Epistemology

Dharmakīrti's Buddhist epistemology (pramāṇavāda) offers an alternative: **valid cognition** (pramāṇa) is of two types — direct perception (pratyakṣa) and inference (anumāna). This is broadly empiricist and anticipates some features of reliabilism, while grounding the account in [[dependent-origination|dependent origination]] rather than a correspondence theory of truth.
