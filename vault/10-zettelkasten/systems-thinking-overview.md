# Systems Thinking — A Primer

Systems thinking is a discipline for seeing wholes rather than parts. Instead of isolating variables and studying them in series (reductionism), systems thinking examines **the relationships and feedback loops** that determine behaviour over time.

## Key Concepts

### Feedback Loops
- **Reinforcing (positive) loops** amplify change: compound interest, population growth, viral spread. Left unchecked, they produce exponential growth or collapse.
- **Balancing (negative) loops** resist change and maintain stability: a thermostat, predator-prey dynamics, homeostasis in living bodies.

Most real systems are webs of both types. Behaviour emerges from **the interaction of loops**, not from any single cause.

### Stocks and Flows
A **stock** is any accumulation: water in a bathtub, trust in a relationship, CO₂ in the atmosphere. A **flow** fills or drains a stock. Understanding a system requires knowing what the stocks are and what controls the flows into and out of them — not just the immediately visible events.

### Delays
Delays between cause and effect are a primary source of oscillation and policy resistance. Acting before feedback has arrived produces overshoot; the classic example is a shower that alternates between scalding and freezing because the temperature lag is longer than the adjustment time.

## Gregory Bateson

Bateson unified systems thinking, cybernetics, and ecology in *Steps to an Ecology of Mind* (1972). His core claim: **mind is not located in the brain but in the pattern of relationships** — it is an emergent property of any sufficiently complex feedback system. This resonates deeply with [[dependent-origination]] and [[second-order-cybernetics]].
