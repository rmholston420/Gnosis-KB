# Dependent Origination (Pratītyasamutpāda)

Pratītyasamutpāda — translated as dependent origination, dependent arising, or conditioned co-production — is one of the central doctrines of Buddhist philosophy. It states that **all phenomena arise in dependence upon conditions; nothing exists independently or intrinsically**.

The classical formulation:
> *"When this is, that is.
> This arising, that arises.
> When this isn't, that isn't.
> This ceasing, that ceases."*
— Majjhima Nikāya 79

## The Twelve Nidānas

In Theravāda teaching, the doctrine is elaborated as a chain of twelve links (nidānas) describing the arising of suffering across lifetimes:

1. Ignorance (avidyā)
2. Volitional formations (saṃskāra)
3. Consciousness (vijñāna)
4. Name-and-form (nāmarūpa)
5. Six sense bases (ṣaḍāyatana)
6. Contact (sparśa)
7. Feeling tone (vedanā)
8. Craving (tṛṣṇā)
9. Clinging (upādāna)
10. Becoming (bhava)
11. Birth (jāti)
12. Aging-and-death (jarāmaraṇa)

## Systems Resonance

Dependent origination maps remarkably well onto the concerns of [[second-order-cybernetics]] and [[systems-thinking-overview]]. Both reject isolated, substance-based ontologies in favour of relational accounts of reality. Gregory Bateson's unit of mind — "the difference that makes a difference" — is a secular echo of the nidāna logic: no element has intrinsic properties apart from its place in the network.

## Philosophical Depth

Nāgārjuna's Madhyamaka philosophy radicalises dependent origination into śūnyatā (emptiness): **because everything arises dependently, nothing has svabhāva (own-nature)**. This is not nihilism — empty phenomena are conventionally real and function perfectly — but it dissolves the reified self that is the root of suffering.
