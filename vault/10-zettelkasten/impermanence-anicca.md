# Impermanence (Anicca)

Anicca (Pāli; Sanskrit: *anitya*) — impermanence — is the first of the Three Marks of Existence in Buddhist teaching, alongside dukkha (unsatisfactoriness) and anattā (non-self). It asserts that **all conditioned phenomena are transient**; no compounded thing persists unchanged from one moment to the next.

## Two Levels

**Gross impermanence** is obvious: people age, seasons change, technologies become obsolete. Most traditions acknowledge this. What is distinctive to Buddhist teaching is:

**Subtle impermanence** (kṣaṇikatvā — momentariness): even within what appears as a stable object, there is constant arising and passing away at the sub-experiential level. Abhidharma analysis pushes this to an extreme atomic theory of momentary dharmas.

## Contemplative Practice

Meditating on impermanence is not pessimism — it is a diagnostic tool. Suffering (dukkha) arises when the mind **grasps** at pleasant experiences as permanent or **pushes away** unpleasant ones as intolerable. Seeing clearly that all states are transient loosens the grip of both grasping and aversion.

In Tibetan Buddhism, Lojong (mind training) teachings use anicca as a spur to renunciation and as an antidote to procrastination: *"If I will die — and I will — what truly matters now?"*

## Resonance with Systems Science

Impermanence maps onto the dynamic equilibrium described in [[systems-thinking-overview|systems thinking]]: what appears stable is often a **steady state maintained by flows**, not a static condition. A river appears continuous but its water is constantly new. This is also the logic of [[dependent-origination]]: there is no persistent substance, only the continuity of process.
