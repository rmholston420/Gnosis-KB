# The Zettelkasten Method

The Zettelkasten ("slip-box") method is a personal knowledge management system developed by sociologist Niklas Luhmann, who used it to write more than 70 books and 400 scholarly articles over a 40-year career. The core insight is that **knowledge compounds when notes are linked, not just stored**.

## Core Principles

1. **Atomicity** — each note contains exactly one idea. If a note tries to say two things, split it.
2. **Autonomy** — every note must be understandable in isolation, without context from surrounding notes.
3. **Linkage** — the value of the system comes from explicit [[wikilinks]] between ideas, not from hierarchical folders.
4. **Your own words** — never copy-paste. Restate the idea in your own formulation so understanding is tested at the moment of writing.

## Note Types in This Vault

- **Fleeting notes** (`00-inbox/`) — quick captures, processed within 24 hours
- **Literature notes** (`20-literature/`) — what a source says, in your words
- **Permanent notes** (`10-zettelkasten/`) — your own synthesised ideas, linked richly
- **Maps of Content** (`40-mocs/`) — index notes that curate a topic
- **Projects** (`30-projects/`) — outcome-oriented, temporary

## Why It Works

Most filing systems are **retrieval systems** — you store something to find it later. Zettelkasten is a **thinking system** — you write notes to generate new ideas through unexpected collisions. When you add a new note, you ask: *what does this connect to?* That question forces active synthesis.

Luhmann called his slip-box a "conversation partner." Over time, clusters of interlinked notes begin to suggest arguments, patterns, and research directions that no individual note contains.
