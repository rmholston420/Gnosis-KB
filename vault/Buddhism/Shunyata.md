# Shunyata — Emptiness

*Shunyata* (Tib. *stong pa nyid*) — emptiness — is the ultimate nature of all phenomena: the absence of inherent, independent, self-sufficient existence.  It is the central insight of the Prajnaparamita literature and the philosophical heart of Madhyamaka.

## Nagarjuna's Formulation

Nagarjuna (*c.* 2nd century CE) established the Madhyamaka school on the insight that all phenomena are *pratityasamutpanna* — dependently originated.  Because nothing arises independently, nothing possesses *svabhava* (own-nature or inherent existence).  This is not nihilism: empty phenomena still appear and function conventionally.

> *"Whatever is dependently co-arisen, that is explained to be emptiness.  That, being a dependent designation, is itself the middle way."*  
> — Nagarjuna, *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā* 24:18

## The Two Truths

| Level | Tibetan | Meaning |
|-------|---------|--------|
| Conventional truth | *Kun rdzob bden pa* | How things appear and function |
| Ultimate truth | *Don dam bden pa* | How things actually exist — empty of inherent existence |

The two truths are not contradictory: conventional appearances are possible *because* of emptiness, not despite it.

## Dzogchen Perspective

In the Nyingma Dzogchen tradition emptiness is inseparable from luminosity (*gsal ba*).  Mind's nature is *kadak* (primordially pure, i.e., empty) and *lhundrup* (spontaneously present, i.e., luminous).  Realization is not the attainment of emptiness but the recognition of this already-present nature.
