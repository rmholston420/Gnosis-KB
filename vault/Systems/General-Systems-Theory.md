# General Systems Theory

General Systems Theory (GST), developed primarily by Ludwig von Bertalanffy in the 1940s–1960s, is the transdisciplinary study of abstract organization, independent of the particular substance, type, or spatial/temporal scale of existence.  It proposes that isomorphic principles — patterns that recur across biology, physics, economics, and social organization — can be formalized into a general science of systems.

## Core Principles

### Wholeness and Emergence

A system's properties cannot be reduced to the sum of its parts.  **Emergence** is the appearance of novel properties at higher levels of organization that are not predictable from knowledge of the components alone.  Example: water's wetness is not a property of individual H₂O molecules.

### Open Systems and Equifinality

Living systems are **open systems** — they exchange matter, energy, and information with their environment.  **Equifinality** (Bertalanffy) means that the same final state can be reached from different initial conditions via different paths.  This is in contrast to closed systems where initial conditions determine outcome.

### Feedback and Homeostasis

- **Negative feedback** (*error-correction*) — deviations from a set-point trigger responses that reduce the deviation; the basis of homeostasis and goal-directed behavior
- **Positive feedback** (*amplification*) — deviations are amplified; the basis of growth, learning, and phase transitions

## Cybernetics Connection

Norbert Wiener's cybernetics (1948) provided the mathematical language for feedback and control that GST needed.  The two traditions merged in the Macy Conferences (1946–1953) and converged into systems thinking as practiced today.
