# Cybernetics and Second-Order Thinking

First-order cybernetics (Wiener, 1948) studied observed systems — control and communication in machines and animals.  Second-order cybernetics, developed by Heinz von Foerster and colleagues in the 1970s, turned the lens on the observer: it is the cybernetics of *observing* systems.  The observer is always part of the system being observed.

## The Epistemological Shift

First-order: *The system has properties that the observer measures.*  
Second-order: *The observer's cognitive structure shapes what counts as a system and what properties are perceived.*

This connects to Humberto Maturana's **radical constructivism**: cognition does not recover an external reality but enacts a domain of interactions.  There is no view from nowhere.

## Autopoiesis

Maturana and Varela (1972) described living systems as **autopoietic** — self-producing.  An autopoietic system continuously produces the components that constitute it; its organization is conserved through its own operation.  This is the clearest formal statement of what makes a living system alive.

Key distinction:
- **Organization** (*invariant*) — the relations that define the system's identity
- **Structure** (*variable*) — the physical components that realize the organization at a given moment

## Connection to Buddhist Epistemology

The second-order insight — that the observer cannot step outside the observed — resonates deeply with Madhyamaka: the subject/object split is itself a conventional designation, not a fundamental feature of reality.  The [[Shunyata]] of the observer and the observed are co-dependent.
